Top-Level Findings

STRONGLY VALIDATED: Our core cascade chain (D1→D2→D3→D4→D5→D6→D7) is confirmed by every framework examined. Regulation (D1) as the universal prerequisite has overwhelming convergent evidence from Porges, Schore, Greenspan, Kuypers, Greene, and Mahler.
STRONGLY VALIDATED: The interoception → emotion identification → emotion regulation chain (D1-sa1 → D2-sa1 → D1-sa3/sa4) matches Mahler's curriculum exactly (Body → Emotion → Action) and is confirmed by Zones of Regulation, DIR FEDC 1, and intervention studies.
STRONGLY VALIDATED: Emotion regulation predicting executive function (D1→D3) has direct longitudinal evidence — Halse et al. (2024, n=852) showed ER→EF but NOT EF→ER in school-age children.
STRONGLY VALIDATED: Language preceding theory of mind (communication skills before perspective-taking, our D5 before D6) confirmed by longitudinal research — language at age 3 predicts ToM in adolescence, not reverse.
REFINE: Our 5-tier system maps well to clinical models but the DIR model uses 6+ FEDCs and VB-MAPP uses 3 levels. Our Tier 1 (Reflexive) may be too broad — it contains both truly reflexive responses AND early interoceptive awareness, which some frameworks separate.
REFINE: D9 (Support System Skills) has no direct clinical assessment equivalent. It maps to what Schore describes as the caregiver side of co-regulation and what EFL calls "tolerance/cooperation" but is unique to our framework as a BCBA-facing domain.
GAP: VB-MAPP factor analysis shows skills cluster by developmental complexity, not by verbal operant type. This suggests our tier assignments (complexity-based) are more valid than domain-based groupings, but it also means we should validate that skills WITHIN a sub-area genuinely belong at the same complexity level.

D1: Regulation — Clinical Validation

Strongly Validated

Framework Alignment

DIR/Floortime
FEDC 1 (Self-Regulation and Interest in the World) is the absolute foundation — Greenspan: "the basic level for all of us." Without regulation, engagement with people or activities cannot proceed. Direct match to D1 as Tier 1 foundation.
Zones of Reg.
Interoception explicitly identified as crucial prerequisite to all self-regulation. Mahler's curriculum: Body Lessons (1-16) → Emotion Lessons (17-20) → Action Lessons (21-25). Validates our D1-sa1 → D2-sa1 → D1-sa3 sequencing.
Polyvagal
Porges' 3-level autonomic hierarchy (ventral vagal → sympathetic → dorsal vagal). Neuroception operates below conscious awareness. Validates Tier 1 "reflexive" assignment for D1-sa1 interoception skills.
Attachment
Schore: Right-brain co-regulation is the neurobiological mechanism for developing self-regulation. Co-regulation → autoregulation is the developmental arc. Validates D1 as prerequisite to everything + validates D9 co-regulation focus.
CPS (Greene)
Sensory/motor regulation is the bottom of the lagging skills hierarchy. Regulation failures produce cascading failures in flexibility, EF, social cognition. Validates D1→D3→D4→D6 cascade chain.
Halse 2024
Longitudinal (n=852): Emotion regulation predicts EF development. EF does NOT predict ER. Direction is ER→EF, confirming D1→D3. Direct empirical confirmation of our dependency direction.

Tier Assignment Validation

D1-sa1 (Noticing Internal Signals):

  • sg1 skills (detect physiological changes) at Tier 1 — Validated — Mahler Body Lessons, Polyvagal neuroception, all below conscious awareness
  • sg2 skills (detect arousal/energy) at Tier 1 — Validated — autonomic responses, pre-conscious
  • sg3 skills (attend inward) at Tier 2 — Validated — requires attentional focus (Zones Section 1 "identify")
  • sg4 skills (differentiate/sequence) at Tier 3 — Validated — Mahler Emotion Lessons, Zones Section 2 "detect triggers"

D1-sa3 (Calming Down):

  • sg1 (notice activation) at Tier 1 — Validated — fight/flight is autonomic
  • sg2 (co-regulation) at Tiers 1-2 — Validated — Schore: interactive regulation is the earliest form; tolerating adult proximity is a co-regulation skill
  • sg3 (use tools) at Tiers 2-3 — Validated — Zones Section 2-3: from accepting cues to self-selecting strategies

D1-sa5 (Staying Safe):

  • sg1 (inhibit aggression/SIB) at Tier 1 — Discuss — EFL "Necessary Nine" places these as foundational, but inhibition is an active EF process (Diamond), not reflexive. See Decision Point #1.
Overall D1 verdict: Our tier assignments for D1 align precisely with the Mahler interoception curriculum (Body→Emotion→Action), the Zones 3-section progression, and the DIR FEDC 1 emphasis on regulation as foundation. The escalation from Tier 1 (detect) → Tier 2 (attend/accept cues) → Tier 3 (use strategies) maps to clinical practice.

D3: Executive Function — Clinical Validation

Strongly Validated

Framework Alignment

Diamond
3 core EFs: Inhibitory control, Working memory, Cognitive flexibility. Flexibility requires the other two. Stress impairs EF, confirming D1→D3 dependency. Validates our D3 internal ordering: inhibition (sa1-sa2) → flexibility (sa3) → planning/monitoring (sa4-sa5).
CPS (Greene)
EF lagging skills: transitions, sequencing, persistence, time sense, focus, consequence consideration, solution range, cognitive inflexibility. Almost 1:1 match with our D3 sub-areas.
Halse 2024
ER→EF direction confirmed. At within-person level, improved emotion regulation predicts progressive EF development. EF does NOT predict improved ER. Strongest possible validation of D1→D3 dependency direction.
ABLLS-R
Domain A (Cooperation) comes first — establishing reinforcer effectiveness and cooperation is foundational prerequisite for all instruction. Validates D3-sa1 (Initiation) depending on regulation + motivation.
VB-MAPP
Barriers 11 (Prompt Dependency), 14 (Defective Conditional Discriminations), 22 (Hyperactive Behavior) all map to EF deficits blocking skill acquisition. Validates EF as a gateway skill for learning.

Tier Assignment Validation

  • D3-sa1 (Initiation) at Tiers 2-3 — Validated — Basic action initiation is an early EF skill; "entering tasks without motivation" at Tier 3 requires discomfort tolerance (D1-sa4)
  • D3-sa2 (Persistence) at Tiers 2-3 — Validated — Maps to EFL "completing required tasks" and Greene "persisting on challenging tasks"
  • D3-sa3 (Flexibility) at Tier 3 — Validated — Diamond: flexibility requires inhibitory control + working memory. Greene: cognitive inflexibility is a distinct lagging skill domain
  • D3-sa4 (Planning) at Tiers 3-4 — Validated — Sequential multi-step processing requires working memory (Tier 3), real-time task analysis requires integration (Tier 4)
  • D3-sa5 (Self-Monitoring) at Tier 4 — Validated — Metacognitive monitoring requires representational capacity (DIR FEDC 5). Zones Section 3 self-monitoring is advanced.
  • D3-sa6 (Repairing Mistakes) at Tiers 3-4 — Validated — Requires D1 calming + D2 limits + error tolerance. "Separating error from self-worth" (Tier 4) maps to Harter self-concept development.

Dependency Validation: D3 depends on D1, D2

D1→D3 confirmed by strongest evidence available: Halse et al. (2024) longitudinal study directly tested this and found ER→EF but not EF→ER. Additionally, Diamond's stress-impairs-EF finding means D1 regulation capacity directly gates D3 performance.
D2→D3 confirmed: Self-monitoring (D3-sa5) requires naming feelings (D2-sa1) and recognizing limits (D2-sa4). Greene's model places language processing (expressing needs/thoughts) before EF application.

D5: Communication — Clinical Validation

Validated with Notes

Framework Alignment

VB-MAPP
Verbal operant chain: Echoic → Mand → Tact → LRFFC → Intraverbal. Our D5 focuses on functional communication (requesting help, expressing discomfort, negotiating) rather than verbal operant form. Different framing but compatible — our skills assume some base communication form exists.
DIR/Floortime
FEDC 3 (Two-Way Communication) → FEDC 4 (Complex Communication). Intentional back-and-forth prerequisite to shared problem-solving. Validates D5-sa1 (Requesting Help) and D5-sa4 (Negotiating) as increasingly complex.
CPS (Greene)
"Difficulty expressing concerns, needs, or thoughts in words" is an explicit lagging skill. Language processing precedes cognitive flexibility in the hierarchy. Validates communication skills as prerequisite to higher-order problem solving.
ABLLS-R
Domains F (Requests/Mands), G (Labeling/Tacts), H (Intraverbals) form a progression. Domain A (Cooperation) is prerequisite to all. Validates that manding/requesting is early communication; intraverbal is advanced.

Important Distinction

Note: Our D5 is NOT the VB-MAPP verbal operant hierarchy. Our D5 is about functional use of communication — the pragmatic "what for" rather than the operant "how." A child could have strong tact/mand repertoire (VB-MAPP) but still struggle with D5-sa4 (Negotiating) because negotiation requires perspective-taking + flexibility + discomfort tolerance. Our model correctly positions these as Tier 4 skills.

Tier Assignment Validation

  • D5-sa1 (Requesting Help) at Tiers 2-3 — Validated — Manding is the earliest verbal operant (VB-MAPP Level 1). Basic requesting is Tier 2; knowing WHAT to request is Tier 3.
  • D5-sa2 (Expressing Discomfort) at Tiers 2-4 — Validated — Tacting private events (VB-MAPP Level 2-3). "Using labels instead of behavior" (Tier 3) is a core ABA target.
  • D5-sa3 (Explaining Problems) at Tiers 3-4 — Validated — Intraverbal complexity (VB-MAPP Level 3), cause-effect reasoning
  • D5-sa4 (Negotiating) at Tiers 3-4 — Validated — DIR FEDC 4: complex multi-step communication for negotiating needs
  • D5-sa5 (Advocating) at Tiers 4-5 — Validated — Requires self-knowledge (D7 territory), persistence, and social understanding
  • D5-sa6 (Repairing Miscommunication) at Tiers 3-5 — Validated — Social repair requires perspective-taking (D6) + flexibility (D3)

Decision Points Requiring Your Clinical Judgment

These are areas where the research provides multiple valid approaches or where our model makes choices that diverge from established conventions.

Decision 1: Safety Inhibition Tier Assignment

D1-sa5-sg1 skills (inhibit aggression, SIB, elopement) are currently at Tier 1 (Reflexive). However, behavioral inhibition is an active EF process (Diamond). EFL places these as foundational prerequisites ("Necessary Nine"), but the mechanism is not reflexive — it requires cognitive control.

A Keep at Tier 1 — These are non-negotiable safety behaviors that must be targeted first regardless of cognitive level. The "tier" reflects clinical priority, not developmental complexity.
B Move to Tier 2 — Acknowledge that inhibition requires at least basic attentional focus. This is more developmentally accurate but changes the safety-first messaging.
C Split — Keep "inhibit serious aggression" at Tier 1 (as a survival priority) but move "follow adult safety directives" to Tier 2 (requires listener responding).

Decision 2: Should We Add Joint Attention as a Skill/Sub-Area?

Joint attention (JA) is consistently identified as a behavioral cusp — a pivotal prerequisite that opens access to language, social learning, and ToM. JA at 9-12 months predicts ToM at 4-4.5 years. Currently our framework does NOT explicitly include JA. It's implicitly embedded in D6 (social understanding) and D5 (communication) prerequisites.

A Add JA as a sub-area in D6 — explicit tracking of responding-to-JA, initiating-JA, and coordinated-JA.
B Add JA skills within existing sub-areas — e.g., add "respond to shared gaze/point" in D6-sa3 (Turn-Taking) and "initiate shared attention" in D5-sa1 or D6-sa1.
C Keep implicit — our framework targets the developmental range AFTER joint attention is typically established. JA is a lower developmental range than our Tier 1 skills.

Decision 3: D9 (Support System) — Independent or Dependent?

D9 is currently marked independent (no cross-domain prerequisites). However, Schore's research shows co-regulation (D9) is the mechanism that produces self-regulation (D1). The caregiver's regulation capacity directly shapes the child's regulatory development. Should D9 have a dependency relationship with D1?

A Keep independent — D9 is assessed on the BCBA/caregiver, not the client. It's a different assessment target.
B Make D9→D1 a "support dependency" — not a blocking prerequisite but a clinically important influence arrow shown in the cascade visualization.
C Remove D9 from the cascade model entirely — it's a different assessment paradigm (assessing the environment, not the client).

Decision 4: Should Dependencies Be "Blocking" or "Graded"?

VB-MAPP distinguishes between task analysis skills (strict prerequisites — work on in order) and supporting skills (concurrent development — don't need to be in order). Currently our dependencies are binary: met or unmet. Should we model "supporting" vs "blocking" relationships?

A Keep binary — simpler model, easier to understand and implement. "Blocked" state works well clinically.
B Add dependency weight (0-1) — 1.0 = strict prerequisite (can't proceed), 0.5 = supporting (can proceed but progress will be slower), 0.0 = no dependency.
C Add dependency types — "blocks" (must be met first), "supports" (accelerates progress), "enables" (unlocks but doesn't block).

Decision 5: Barrier/Risk Layer — Add VB-MAPP Barriers?

The VB-MAPP identifies 24 barriers to learning (prompt dependency, scrolling, weak motivators, sensory defensiveness, etc.) that CUT ACROSS domains. These explain WHY a skill isn't progressing. Our current model doesn't have a barrier/risk layer. Should we add one?

A Add a barrier assessment — 10-15 most relevant barriers scored 0-4, mapped to which domains they affect. Show in Risk Monitor view.
B Don't add barriers — our cascade model already captures the "why" through dependency chains (if D3 is weak, that explains why D4 isn't progressing). Barriers add complexity without changing interventions.
C Add barriers later — this is a Phase 2 feature after the core dependency model is validated in practice.
